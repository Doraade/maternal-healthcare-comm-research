About This Research
This research examines the experiences of communication in maternity care among refugee and migrant women in the UK. It focuses on how women understand the way healthcare professionals talk about pain, medical procedures, and consent, and how this communication affects their understanding, feelings and trust during pregnancy and childbirth.
Who is the Researcher?
Name: Dorcas Adebayo
Institution: Liverpool Hope University
Email: 24013036@hope.ac.uk
What Will My Participation Involve?
If you choose to take part, you will be invited to take part in either:
  • A one-to-one interview, or
  • A small focus group discussion.

You will be asked open questions about your experiences of communication with healthcare professionals during pregnancy and childbirth. This includes how information about pain, medical procedures and consent were explained to you, how you understood this information and how it made you feel.

There are no right or wrong answers. You may choose what you want to share. Sessions will last approximately 30–45 minutes (interviews) or 60–90 minutes (focus groups). Discussions will be audio-recorded only with your permission.
Will There Be Any Benefits to Me?
There may be no direct personal benefit to you from taking part. However, some people find it helpful to reflect on their experiences and feel that their voices are being heard.

Moreover, your contribution may help improve understanding of how maternity care communication is experienced by refugee and migrant women, which could support more sensitive and respectful communication practices in the future.
Will There Be Any Risks to Me?
This study involves minimal risk. Some participants may feel emotional discomfort when talking about personal experiences of pregnancy and childbirth.

You do not have to answer any questions that make you uncomfortable. You may pause, skip questions, or stop the discussion at any time. If you feel upset, the session can be stopped and you will be offered information about support services if needed.
What If I Want to Withdraw?
Taking part is completely voluntary. You are free to:
  • Stop taking part at any time
  • Withdraw from the study without giving a reason
  • Ask for your information to be removed up to two weeks after participation

After this time, it may not be possible to remove your information because it will have been anonymised. Choosing not to take part or withdrawing will not affect any healthcare or support services you currently receive.
How Will My Contribution Be Kept Anonymous?
Your privacy will be protected at all times:
  • Your name will not appear in any transcripts or reports.
  • A pseudonym (a made-up name or number) will be used instead of your real name.
  • Any details that could identify you (such as specific locations or people) will be removed or changed.
  • All information will be stored securely and only accessed by the researcher and academic supervisors.
  • No information that could identify you will be included in the thesis, reports, or publications.
Please note: Your confidentiality and anonymity cannot be assured if, during the research, it comes to light that you are involved in illegal or harmful behaviours which the researcher may need to disclose to the appropriate authorities.
